Police Source Data This page is about answering what I think is the most important question of this project: can data projects get the police source data they need? When I say police source data, I am referring to the raw data that is collected by police departments that ideally, people can access to perform various analyses on. Examples of data projects using various forms of policing data can be seen on the Data Project Spotlight page.

Below I outline my process and an overview of the results of my searching experience. But you can also skip straight to the acquiring data section if you would just like to read the raw information on what it is like to obtain this data.

Process

When I went to answer the question of police data accessibility, my goal was to find answers to a few key questions:

  1. What is the policy regarding obtaining policing data? I.e. How should it work in theory according to legislation?
  2. What is the real world experience of data projects, and data scientists, when gathering data for data advocacy projects?
  3. And, related to the above, what is the quality of the data once (/if) obtained?

These three questions were chosen to help provide a broad overview of what the state of police data accessibility is like. I also believed that answering these questions would cover the most commonly asked questions that I get when I bring up my work using policing data. I donâ€™t have all the answers, even though I do that work, and I wanted to find at least the beginnings of them.

For finding answers, I conducted a number of searches to draw on a broad array of potential sources. These sources included database searches for academic papers and articles, data advocacy projects, articles written by data scientists doing this work, legal resources about policy, government databases, and official government websites on legislation and records requests processes.

Results in Short

I found a number of large data advocacy projects that included insightful information on their records request experiences. This combined with articles written by people doing this work and the exploration of government data portals allowed me to confidently provide perspective on questions #2 and #3.

Hoever, I was ultimately unable to find the kinds of information I really desired to answer the questions regarding police data acquisition policy. While I was able to find ample information on federal data processes (under FOIA, more information below), I was unable to find reputable, up-to-date, and applicable sources for the state legislation which covers policing data (often called FOI legislation, varies significantly state to state).
